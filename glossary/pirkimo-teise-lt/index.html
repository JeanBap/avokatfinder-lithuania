Pirkimo Pirmumo Teisė

Pirkimo pirmumo teisė is the right of pre-emption in Lithuanian law that entitles specific parties — co-owners, tenants, or the state — to purchase a property before the owner may complete a sale to a third party at the same price and terms.

Lithuanian property law (Civil Code, Book IV) recognises statutory pirkimo pirmumo teisė (right of first purchase or pre-emption right) that must be honoured before completing a sale to an external buyer. The right ensures that parties with a legitimate interest in the property have the opportunity to acquire it before an outsider does.

Co-owners of undivided property (bendroji dalinė nuosavybė) have a statutory pre-emption right. If one co-owner wishes to sell their share, they must formally notify the other co-owners of the intended sale price and terms. Co-owners have one month to exercise the right at the same price. Failure to notify allows the bypassed co-owner to apply to court to be substituted as the buyer.

Tenants in residential properties may hold a pre-emption right under specific conditions, particularly for tenants of state-owned housing being privatised or for long-term tenants under specific protection rules. The exact scope depends on the nature of the tenancy and applicable legislation.

The Lithuanian state holds pirkimo pirmumo teisė over cultural heritage properties and certain protected nature reserve parcels. Before selling, the owner must notify the relevant state authority, which may exercise the right within the statutory period at the agreed market price.

Agricultural land transactions are subject to additional pre-emption rights under the Law on Land, with restrictions on who may purchase agricultural land and priority rights for neighbouring landowners and agricultural businesses. These rules have been a source of legal uncertainty and were actively reviewed in the 2020s.

⚠ Common MistakeSellers of co-owned property sometimes notify co-owners informally by phone or message, without a formal written notice. An informal notification does not start the statutory one-month period. A sale completed without proper notification can be challenged in court by the overlooked co-owner.
💡 Expert TipBefore buying any co-owned property share, ask for documentary evidence that all other co-owners have received formal written notification and that their one-month period has elapsed or they have explicitly waived the right in writing. Keep this documentation permanently as proof against future claims.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the pre-emption right be waived contractually in advance?

Statutory pre-emption rights generally cannot be waived in advance. They must be triggered, exercised, or allowed to expire in each transaction.

What if the property is sold at a lower price after the pre-emption notification?

The sale to a third party must be at the same or higher price than offered to the right-holder. Selling at a lower price after the right-holder declines triggers a new notification obligation.

Does pirktimo pirmumo teise apply to inheritance?

Pre-emption rights are triggered by a voluntary sale, not by inheritance. Inherited property passes according to succession law without triggering co-owners' pre-emption rights.
