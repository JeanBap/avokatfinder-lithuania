Bendroji Dalinė Nuosavybė

Bendroji dalinė nuosavybė is the Lithuanian civil law regime of shared fractional ownership of property, under which two or more persons each hold a defined proportional share of an undivided asset, with rights and restrictions on disposal, use, and management.

Under the Lithuanian Civil Code (Book IV, Property Law), bendroji dalinė nuosavybė (co-ownership with defined shares) arises when two or more persons jointly own a property and each holds a specified fraction — for example, one half, one third, or any other proportion. This differs from joint ownership (bendroji jungtinė nuosavybė), where shares are not individually defined, which applies to marital property.

Each co-owner in a dalinė nuosavybė relationship may independently dispose of their own share — sell it, mortgage it, or give it away — subject to the pre-emption right (pirkimo pirmumo teisė) of the other co-owners. No co-owner may unilaterally dispose of the entire undivided property without the consent of all others.

Management of the commonly owned property requires agreement among co-owners. Ordinary management decisions require a majority by ownership share; extraordinary decisions (major reconstruction, lease exceeding one year, sale of the whole property) require unanimity. Disputes that cannot be resolved by agreement may be referred to court.

Any co-owner may at any time request partition of the jointly owned property, either by physical division (if divisible) or by compulsory sale and distribution of proceeds. If the parties cannot agree on partition terms, the court orders the method and terms. This right to demand partition cannot be permanently waived.

Buyers considering purchasing one co-owner's share in an undivided property should proceed with caution. They will not gain exclusive possession of any specific area of the property and will need to co-operate with the remaining co-owners on all management decisions. They may also face a court-ordered partition application from the other co-owners if relations deteriorate.

⚠ Common MistakeBuyers purchasing a co-owner's share sometimes assume they will have exclusive use of a specific part of the property. Without a formal partition or a co-use agreement, all co-owners have equal rights to the entire property. Expect disputes if co-use is not agreed in writing at the time of purchase.
💡 Expert TipIf you are buying a share in co-owned property, simultaneously negotiate a detailed co-ownership agreement (bendraturčių sutartis) setting out which areas each co-owner uses exclusively, how costs are shared, and what happens if a co-owner wants to sell. Register this agreement in the Real Property Register.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between daline and jungtine nuosavybe?

Dalinė nuosavybė is co-ownership with defined shares (e.g. 50%/50%). Jungtinė nuosavybė is joint ownership without defined shares — typically marital property where both spouses own everything jointly.

Can a co-owner mortgage their share?

Yes, but a mortgage on one co-owner's undivided share is less attractive to lenders than a mortgage on a whole property. The lender's security would cover only that fraction.

What happens to co-ownership when one co-owner dies?

The deceased co-owner's share passes to their heirs through succession (paveldėjimas). The heirs become co-owners in place of the deceased. This can increase the number of co-owners in a property over generations.
