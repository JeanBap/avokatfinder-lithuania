Notarinis Sandoris

A notarinis sandoris is a notarially authenticated legal transaction in Lithuania — the mandatory form for all property sale deeds, mortgage agreements, and gifts of immovable property — ensuring binding legal effect and enabling registration in the Real Property Register.

Lithuanian law requires that all agreements transferring ownership of immovable property, establishing mortgages, or gifting real estate be concluded in notarial form (notarinė forma). A written contract not authenticated by a notary has no legal effect for immovable property. The notary authenticates the parties' identities, explains the legal consequences, and archives the original deed.

Lithuanian notaries are public officers regulated by the Chamber of Notaries (Lietuvos notarų rūmai) and the Law on Notaries. They are geographically unbound — any Lithuanian notary may authenticate a property transaction regardless of where the property is located. Parties may freely choose their notary.

The notarial deed for a property sale records the parties' personal data (name, personal code, address), the property's unique register number, purchase price, payment method, delivery of possession, and any conditions. After signing, the notary submits the registration application to the Real Property Register electronically. The transfer is typically registered within 3–5 working days.

For preliminary purchase agreements (preliminarios sutartys), notarial form is not legally required but is strongly recommended. A notarially authenticated preliminary agreement is specifically enforceable — a court can compel completion of the sale. A simple written preliminary agreement may not be specifically enforceable for real property.

Cross-border transactions are facilitated by powers of attorney (įgaliojimai) authenticated by Lithuanian consulates abroad or by foreign notaries with apostille. Lithuania also increasingly uses digital tools — lawyers can prepare draft deeds in advance and parties review them digitally before the signing appointment, reducing the time in the notary's office.

⚠ Common MistakeSome buyers sign only a private written preliminary agreement and pay a large deposit, believing this creates a fully binding obligation. A private written agreement cannot be specifically enforced for real property in Lithuania. The seller can breach it and face only a damages claim, not compulsion to complete the sale.
💡 Expert TipAlways conclude a notarially authenticated preliminary agreement if you need to pay a substantial deposit before the final deed signing. Include a specific performance clause, a completion longstop date, and a deposit-forfeiture or double-return mechanism to protect both parties.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sign the notarial deed remotely without attending in person?

Yes, via a notarially authenticated power of attorney (igaliojimas). If you are abroad, a Lithuanian consulate or a foreign notary with apostille can authenticate the power.

How much does a notarial deed for a property sale cost?

Notarial fees are regulated by the Law on Notaries and calculated on a sliding scale based on the transaction value. The rate decreases as value increases. Additional fees apply for registration, translations, and ancillary services.

Who chooses the notary — the buyer or the seller?

By convention in Lithuania, the buyer typically selects the notary, as the buyer is paying and benefiting most from the authentication. However, parties may agree otherwise.
